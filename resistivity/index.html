Electrical Resistivity / VES Surveys in Coquitlam

Coquitlam sits at the foot of the Coast Mountains, where glacial till and outwash deposits dominate the subsurface. With annual precipitation exceeding 1,800 mm on the slopes of Burke Mountain, groundwater saturation becomes a critical variable for any foundation design. Electrical resistivity surveys—specifically Vertical Electrical Sounding—allow us to map these saturated zones, bedrock depth, and clay lenses without breaking ground. It is a non-invasive method that measures how subsurface materials resist an applied current. In a municipality where terrain rises from sea level to over 600 meters in less than five kilometers, the contrast between dense lodgement till and water-filled granular layers is stark. Our field teams pair a Syscal Pro switch unit with a Wenner-Schlumberger array to reach investigation depths of up to 60 meters. For projects near the Coquitlam River or the Fraser River floodplain, we often combine VES with a CPT test to calibrate resistivity values against direct penetration data, especially where the soil profile transitions from sand to soft silty clay.

Resistivity contrasts in Coquitlam's glacial stratigraphy can exceed 10:1 between saturated silt and intact till—resolving that boundary is what prevents over-excavation on site.

Approach and scope

The field setup for a resistivity sounding in Coquitlam is configured around a central point, expanding electrode spacing in logarithmic steps. We use stainless steel stakes driven 30 cm into the ground, with a 250-watt transmitter injecting a controlled direct current. The Syscal Pro measures the resulting potential difference with a precision of 1 microvolt, stacking multiple readings to filter out the 60 Hz noise from the nearby BC Hydro transmission corridors. What we see most often in this region is a four-layer model: a thin high-resistivity surface layer of dry gravel, a conductive low-resistivity silt-clay unit from the Capilano Sediments, a glacial till with intermediate values, and then a sharp rise in resistivity at the bedrock interface. Data inversion runs through RES2DINV software using a smoothness-constrained least-squares algorithm, producing a 2D pseudo-section that highlights any vertical fractures or perched water tables common in the Westwood Plateau area.

Site-specific factors

The North Shore climate introduces a specific challenge for resistivity work: prolonged rain can create a saturated surface layer that shorts the electrodes and masks deeper signals. Between October and March, Coquitlam receives over 200 mm of rain per month, so we schedule surveys strategically and use bentonite-enhanced electrode contact to maintain current injection. Another factor here is the presence of glacially overridden sediments with very low resistivity—values below 10 ohm-m in some clay units near Como Lake—which can screen out deeper bedrock returns. In these conditions, we increase the maximum electrode spread to 250 meters and apply a higher stacking count per reading. Urban noise from the SkyTrain Evergreen Extension and the Trans-Canada Highway corridor also requires notch filtering at 60 Hz during data acquisition. Despite these constraints, the method remains the most efficient way to map depth-to-bedrock across a large industrial lot without mobilizing a drill rig.

Related technical services

01

1D Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES)

A single-point sounding with expanding AB/2 spacing, ideal for mapping groundwater table depth, bedrock contact, and clay layer thickness beneath a proposed building footprint. We process the apparent resistivity curve through an iterative inversion to produce a layered model calibrated to any available borehole logs from the site.

02

2D Electrical Resistivity Tomography (ERT)

A multi-electrode profile typically 200 to 400 meters long with a fixed electrode spacing of 5 meters. This setup resolves lateral variations along a line, making it suitable for landslide investigation on Burke Mountain slopes, detection of abandoned mine workings, or delineation of a contaminant plume boundary in granular aquifers.

Quick answers

What depth of investigation can a VES survey achieve in Coquitlam's glacial soils?

With a maximum AB/2 spacing of 100 meters, we typically reach 50 to 60 meters of effective depth. The actual penetration depends on the resistivity contrast between layers; a conductive clay cap at 20 meters will attenuate the signal and may reduce the depth of investigation below the theoretical maximum.

How does rain affect the quality of an electrical resistivity survey?

Surface saturation lowers the contact resistance at the electrode-soil interface, which is actually beneficial for current injection. However, if the entire near-surface becomes a continuous conductive layer, it can mask subtle resistivity changes in the upper two meters. We counter this by using a dipole-dipole array with a shallow investigation depth, or by waiting 48 hours after heavy rain for the surface to drain.

Can resistivity data distinguish between clean sand and silty sand?

Not directly. Both clean sand and silty sand can exhibit overlapping resistivity ranges, especially when saturated with fresh water. The method responds to pore-fluid conductivity and clay content more than grain size distribution. We recommend pairing resistivity with a grain size analysis from a test pit or borehole sample to resolve that ambiguity.

How long does field work take for a resistivity survey?

A single VES sounding with manual electrode deployment takes about 45 to 60 minutes on flat terrain. A full 400-meter ERT line with 81 electrodes can be completed in three to four hours, including setup and takedown. Sites with dense vegetation or steep slopes on Burke Mountain add time for cable routing and stake installation.
