When a Coquitlam site presents thick compressible layers and the structural loads demand more than isolated footings can deliver, the conversation naturally turns to raft and mat foundations. Our team has worked on numerous projects across the city where the transition from glacial till to softer sediments—common near the Coquitlam River and Como Lake lowlands—makes uniform support a challenge. A well-designed mat foundation bridges these transitions by distributing column loads across a continuous slab, reducing differential settlement on variable ground. We routinely combine this approach with a deep excavation support analysis when the raft requires a basement level in tighter urban lots, ensuring the excavation remains stable before the mat is even poured. Our design process pulls directly from site-specific geotechnical data, never from textbook assumptions.

Mat foundation design in Coquitlam is less about the slab thickness and more about mapping how the subgrade stiffness changes metre by metre across the site.

Approach and scope

One mistake we see repeatedly is specifying a uniform mat thickness for a site where the subsurface stiffness changes by over 30% within the building footprint. This almost always leads to tilting or cracking at the transition zone, even when total settlement stays within code limits. In Coquitlam, where glacially overridden deposits can sit beside post-glacial organics within the same lot, getting the subgrade modulus right becomes the single most important step. Our design workflow starts with mapping the subgrade reaction coefficient across the entire footprint using data from CPT soundings and triaxial tests, then feeds that variable spring bed into a finite element model. We also factor in the seismic demand from NBCC 2020, particularly for sites east of the Port Mann where the site class can shift from C to D within a few hundred metres. For projects where the mat bears on improved ground, we often coordinate with stone column installation to stiffen the soil mass before finalizing the slab design.

Site-specific factors

Coquitlam’s growth from a mill town into a dense suburban hub has pushed development onto land that previous generations avoided—low-lying areas near the Coquitlam and Pitt Rivers, and slopes above the Fraser where colluvium masks bedrock. The geotechnical consequence is that many of today’s building sites carry a legacy of undocumented fill, buried organics, or loose alluvial sands that can liquefy under the design earthquake. When a mat foundation is placed over these materials without addressing the underlying risk, the slab can experience sudden loss of bearing during a seismic event. Our analysis always includes a liquefaction screening based on CPT data, and if the factor of safety drops below 1.1 we work with the structural engineer to either deepen the mat or specify ground improvement before the pour.

Related technical services

01

Subgrade modulus mapping

We derive the modulus of subgrade reaction across the entire building footprint using CPT correlations and, where warranted, in-situ plate load tests to capture the real stiffness variation.

02

Finite element soil-structure interaction

Using variable spring beds in FEM software, we model the raft slab response under dead, live, and seismic load combinations per NBCC 2020.

03

Liquefaction and settlement analysis

For sites with loose saturated sands, we run NCEER-based liquefaction triggering analysis and calculate post-shaking settlement to confirm mat performance.

04

Construction-phase QA/QC support

We provide subgrade inspection, proof rolling observations, and concrete pour documentation to verify that the as-built mat matches the design assumptions.

Quick answers

At what point does a mat foundation make more sense than isolated footings in Coquitlam?

When the combined footing area exceeds roughly 50% of the total building footprint, or when differential settlement between adjacent columns becomes difficult to control due to variable soil stiffness. Coquitlam sites near Burke Mountain or the Fraser River floodplain often hit this threshold early because the soil profile changes rapidly over short distances.

What does a raft foundation design typically cost for a residential or commercial project in Coquitlam?

For the geotechnical design portion—including subgrade characterization, modulus mapping, FEM analysis, and a stamped design report—our fees in Coquitlam generally range from CA$1,360 to CA$5,830 depending on the building footprint size and the complexity of the soil profile. Structural engineering of the slab reinforcement is a separate scope.

How do you account for the seismic demand in the raft design?

We follow the NBCC 2020 site classification and spectral acceleration values for the specific Coquitlam location. The soil-structure interaction model includes the seismic load combinations from Part 4 of the code, and we run a liquefaction screening whenever saturated sands are present within the influence depth of the mat.
